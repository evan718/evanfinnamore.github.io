Understanding Grief and Loss: Evidence-Based Approaches

Understanding Grief

Grief is a natural response to loss—whether from the death of a loved one, the end of a relationship, or significant life changes. It is a personal process that varies from person to person.

Key Points About Grief:

  • There is no "right" way to grieve
  • Grief reactions vary widely among individuals
  • Grieving is a personal, non-linear process
  • Healing is possible without forgetting

The Dual Process Model of Grief

The Dual Process Model (DPM) suggests that healthy grieving involves oscillating between confronting the loss and engaging in restorative activities that help rebuild life after loss.

Dual Process Model

DPM in Practice:

Loss-Oriented Activities:

  • Looking at photos or mementos
  • Visiting places of significance
  • Expressing emotions about the loss

Restoration-Oriented Activities:

  • Learning new skills
  • Forming new relationships
  • Creating new routines

Meaning Reconstruction Model

This approach focuses on how individuals rebuild meaning after experiencing loss, integrating the experience into their life narrative.

Three Key Processes:

  • Sense Making: Understanding the loss within your worldview
  • Benefit Finding: Discovering growth or positive changes resulting from loss
  • Identity Change: Adapting your self-concept post-loss

Meaning-Making Activities:

  • Journaling about your experiences
  • Creating memory books or projects
  • Participating in memorial or legacy projects

Complicated Grief Treatment (CGT)

CGT is an evidence-based intervention for persistent, debilitating grief.

Signs of Complicated Grief:

  • Persistent intense longing for the deceased
  • Difficulty accepting the loss
  • Inability to resume daily activities
  • Extended functional impairment

CGT Components:

  • Processing the narrative of the loss
  • Living with reminders
  • Connecting with memories
  • Envisioning a positive future

Cultural Considerations

Grief rituals and expressions vary across cultures, influencing the grieving process.

Cultural Aspects of Grief:

  • Different mourning rituals
  • Varied beliefs about death and the afterlife
  • Norms regarding expression of grief
  • Community support roles

Self-Care During Grief

Evidence-Based Self-Care Strategies:

  • Physical: Maintain sleep, nutrition, and gentle exercise
  • Emotional: Express your feelings through creative outlets
  • Social: Stay connected with supportive people
  • Practical: Set small, achievable goals

When to Seek Professional Help

If grief feels overwhelming or interferes with daily life, consider professional support.

  • Grief is persistent and unrelenting
  • Thoughts of self-harm arise
  • Daily functioning is significantly impaired
